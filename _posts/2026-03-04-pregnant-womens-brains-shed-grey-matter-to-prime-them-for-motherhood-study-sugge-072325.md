A new study has found that pregnancy leads to measurable structural changes in the brain, which researchers believe may help prepare women for motherhood. The findings challenge common stereotypes about cognitive function during pregnancy.

Scientists conducted brain scans on dozens of women before and after pregnancy. The research indicates a reduction in grey matter volume in specific regions of the brain. According to the researchers, this change is not a sign of cognitive decline, but rather a targeted, adaptive process. They propose it may enhance a mother's ability to recognize her baby's needs and respond to social cues, thereby facilitating bonding and caregiving.

The study's authors suggest the term "baby brain" is an unhelpful cliché that does not accurately reflect these purposeful neurological adaptations observed during pregnancy.

Key points:
- Brain scans show pregnancy is linked to a reduction of grey matter in specific areas.
- Researchers propose this is an adaptive change to improve caregiving abilities.
- The study argues against the simplistic "baby brain" stereotype of cognitive decline.
