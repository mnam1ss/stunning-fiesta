Longtime BBC Radio host Nicky Campbell has shared observations on the nature of public discourse, drawing from his fifty years of experience moderating call-in shows and debates. In a recent reflection, he highlighted a shift in how people engage with differing viewpoints, suggesting that an unwavering conviction in one's own rightness can be more damaging to constructive conversation than the disagreements themselves. Campbell's commentary points to a climate where listening has diminished, replaced by a tendency to dismiss opposing perspectives outright.

The broadcaster did not attribute this trend to any single issue or political side, but rather discussed it as a broader societal pattern observed over his lengthy career. His insights come amid ongoing public and media discussions about polarization and the challenges of civil debate.

Key points:
* A veteran broadcaster with five decades of experience has analyzed changes in public debate.
* The commentary suggests that absolute certainty, rather than disagreement, may hinder productive discourse.
* The observations focus on a general decline in listening and a rise in dismissive communication.
