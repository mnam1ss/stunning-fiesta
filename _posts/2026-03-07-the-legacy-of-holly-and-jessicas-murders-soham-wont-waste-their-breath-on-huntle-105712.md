Two decades after the murders of schoolgirls Holly Wells and Jessica Chapman, the community of Soham, Cambridgeshire, continues to grapple with the lasting impact of the 2002 crime. The girls’ deaths, for which school caretaker Ian Huntley was convicted, remain a defining event for the village. Local reports indicate a collective focus on honoring the victims’ memories rather than on the perpetrator, with many residents expressing a deliberate choice not to discuss him. Community resilience and support networks have been emphasized in the years since, though the profound trauma of the event is acknowledged as a persistent part of the area's history. The case led to significant changes in UK child protection policies and vetting procedures for those working with children.

Key points:
• The 2002 murders of Holly Wells and Jessica Chapman remain a pivotal event for Soham.
• The community reports a sustained focus on memorializing the girls rather than on the convicted offender.
• The case prompted major reforms in child safety and employment vetting systems in the United Kingdom.
