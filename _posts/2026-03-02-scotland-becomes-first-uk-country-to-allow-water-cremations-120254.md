Scotland has approved a new method for handling human remains, becoming the first nation in the United Kingdom to permit water cremation. The process, known as alkaline hydrolysis, will be available as a third option alongside traditional burial and flame-based cremation.

The technique involves placing the body in a pressurized vessel with a solution of water and potassium hydroxide. This combination, heated to a high temperature, accelerates natural decomposition, leaving behind bone ash and a sterile liquid. Proponents note the method uses less energy and results in lower carbon emissions compared to standard cremation.

The Scottish government has introduced regulations to allow licensed operators to offer the service, following consultations on environmental and ethical considerations. The change provides an alternative for families making end-of-life arrangements.

Key points:
* Scotland is the first UK nation to legalize water cremation, or alkaline hydrolysis.
* The process uses a heated water and chemical solution to break down remains.
* It will be offered as an alternative to burial and traditional cremation.
