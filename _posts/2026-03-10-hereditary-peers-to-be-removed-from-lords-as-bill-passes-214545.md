A bill to end the automatic right of hereditary peers to sit in the House of Lords has been approved by Parliament. The legislation will abolish the 92 seats currently reserved for individuals who inherit their titles, a centuries-old tradition. The change marks a significant step in the ongoing reform of the UK's upper parliamentary chamber.

The bill received final approval in the House of Commons and is now expected to receive Royal Assent. Supporters of the measure argue it modernizes the legislature by removing membership based solely on birthright. The hereditary peers will no longer have voting rights or the ability to participate in legislative proceedings once the law takes effect.

Key points:
- The new law removes the 92 seats reserved for hereditary peers in the House of Lords.
- Membership in the upper chamber will no longer be based on inherited titles.
- The bill represents a major structural change to the UK's parliamentary system.
